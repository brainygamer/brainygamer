Design a Side Quest
for The Witcher 3

A Quest Pitch for a World Where Nothing Is Black and White

✦

Overview

The Continent Has Rules. Your Quest Must Follow Them.

The Witcher 3: Wild Hunt is not just a game — it is a richly constructed fictional world with its own internal logic, history, and moral texture. Side quests in The Witcher 3 are rarely filler; they illuminate something about the game's themes: the cost of neutrality, the ambiguity of justice, the collision of cultures, the way ordinary people are ground up by powers beyond their understanding.

Working in pairs, you will design an original side quest that could plausibly exist within that world. Your quest should feel like it was written by the same creative team that made the game — not dropped in from outside.

Evil is evil. Lesser, greater, middling — makes no difference. The degree is arbitrary, the margin negotiable.

The Assignment

Your Quest Design Must Include Five Elements

World & Lore Fit

Situate your quest clearly within the game's setting — region, era, social context. Who is your NPC, and why do they matter? What does your quest say, even quietly, about one of the game's larger themes: the persecution of non-humans, the legacy of war, the limits of the Witcher's neutrality, or the gap between law and justice?

Launch Trigger

How does the player find or activate this quest? Describe the specific trigger: a notice board posting, a location discovered by exploration, a time of day, a required story chapter, a prior relationship with an NPC, or some other condition. Be precise — Witcher 3 quests have specific, often elegant triggers that reward attention and curiosity.

Quest Structure

Walk us through the quest beat by beat. What does the player do? What choices, if any, are presented? Where does moral complexity enter — because it always does in The Witcher 3? Your quest must involve at least two distinct phases (e.g., investigation → confrontation → judgment; or contract → discovery → impossible choice) and at least one decision point with meaningful consequences.

Outcomes & Consequences

Define what the different endings look like — The Witcher 3 rarely offers clean victories. What does the player receive (crowns, a unique item, a relationship change, a journal entry that lingers)? Define at least two distinct outcomes based on player choices, and explain why neither feels entirely satisfying. That discomfort is the point.

Visual & Audio Materials

You must include at least three visual or audio elements to help communicate your quest design. Think of this as a pitch document — something you'd show a development team. Options include:

  • A hand-drawn or digitally created map marking the trigger location and key quest beats
  • Annotated screenshots from the game showing comparable mechanics or locations you're drawing from
  • A mood board capturing the visual tone of your quest (region, weather, time of day)
  • A short audio clip or playlist representing the musical atmosphere
  • Concept sketches of your NPC or key location
  • A storyboard of a key cinematic or dialogue moment

Evaluation Criteria

Your Quest Will Be Judged on Four Dimensions

Lore Coherence

Does it feel authentically Witcher 3? Does it engage meaningfully with the game's themes and world?

Mechanical Plausibility

Could this quest actually work within the game's existing systems — dialogue, investigation, combat, choices?

Narrative Craft

Is the storytelling compelling? Are the characters interesting? Does the quest have genuine moral weight and an ambiguous payoff?

Presentation Quality

Are your visual and audio materials purposeful and well-integrated — not merely decorative?

Deliverable

Present Your Pitch to the Class

Present your quest design using PowerPoint, Google Slides, or any presentation software you prefer. Your presentation should run approximately 10–12 minutes and walk the class through each element of your quest as if you were pitching it to a development team.

Practical note: You will present from the classroom PC, so plan ahead. Save your file to Box, email it to yourself, or upload it to Google Drive. If your presentation includes audio or video, test it in advance — embedded media can behave differently across machines. A backup PDF export is always a smart precaution.
