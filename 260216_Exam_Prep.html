SLIDE 04 // CONCEPTS

Content vs. Form

THE WHAT

The Content

The “message” of the film. Includes plot points, character motivations, dialogue, and sociopolitical themes.

vs
THE HOW

The Form

The cinematic “architecture.” The specific technical choices—like lighting, lenses, and editing—that shape our perception.

SLIDE 05 // VISUALS

Visual Language

MISE-EN-SCÈNE VS. CINEMATOGRAPHY
SLIDE 06 // THE STAGE

Mise-en-Scène: The Stage

Everything placed in front of the camera lens. If it exists physically on set, it belongs to mise-en-scène.

Settings & Props The physical world.
Costume & Makeup Visual characterization.
Blocking Staging actors in space.
Lighting On-set light quality/direction.
SLIDE 07 // THE LENS

Cinematography: The Lens

The art of recording the physical (virtual?) world. It focuses on how the camera interprets the mise-en-scène.

Framing Shot scale (Close-up vs. Long).
Angles Low, High, or Canted.
Movement Pans, Tracking, or Handheld.
Lens/Focus Depth of field and shifts.

SLIDE 09 // CASE STUDY
Visualizing Isolation

Case Study: Banshees of Inisherin

Cinematography

McDonagh uses Extreme Long Shots to dwarf characters against the Irish landscape, emphasizing existential loneliness.

Mise-en-Scène

The staging of characters within a cramped, dimly lit pub contrasts with the vast coast, mirroring the struggle between intimacy and isolation.
