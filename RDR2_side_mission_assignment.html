Design a Side Mission
for Red Dead Redemption 2

A Mission Pitch for a World on the Edge of Extinction

✦

Overview

The World Has Rules. Your Mission Must Follow Them.

Red Dead Redemption 2 is not just a game — it is a richly constructed fictional world with its own internal logic, history, and moral texture. Side missions in RDR 2 are rarely filler; they illuminate something about the game's themes: the death of the frontier, the cost of loyalty, the tension between civilization and wildness, the ambiguity of justice.

Working in pairs, you will design an original side mission that could plausibly exist within that world. Your mission should feel like it was written by the same creative team that made the game — not dropped in from outside.

We're thieves in a world that don't want us no more.

The Assignment

Your Mission Design Must Include Five Elements

World & Lore Fit

Situate your mission clearly within the game's setting — region, era, social context. Who is your NPC, and why do they matter? What does your mission say, even quietly, about one of the game's larger themes?

Launch Trigger

How does the player find or activate this mission? Describe the specific trigger: a location on the map, a time of day, a required story chapter, a prior relationship with an NPC, or some other condition. Be precise — RDR 2 missions have specific, often elegant triggers that reward exploration and attention.

Mission Structure

Walk us through the mission beat by beat. What does the player do? What choices, if any, are presented? Where do things go wrong — because they always do in RDR 2? Your mission must involve at least two distinct gameplay phases (e.g., conversation → tracking → confrontation; or investigation → escort → moral decision).

Success & Fail States

Define what success looks like and what the player receives (honor points, money, a unique item, a relationship change). Define at least one fail state: what triggers failure, and does the mission reset or end permanently?

Visual & Audio Materials

You must include at least three visual or audio elements to help communicate your mission design. Think of this as a pitch document — something you'd show a development team. Options include:

  • A hand-drawn or digitally created map marking the trigger location and key beats
  • Annotated screenshots from the game showing comparable mechanics or locations you're drawing from
  • A mood board capturing the visual tone of your mission
  • A short audio clip or playlist representing the musical atmosphere
  • Concept sketches of your NPC or key location
  • A storyboard of a key cinematic moment

Evaluation Criteria

Your Mission Will Be Judged on Four Dimensions

Lore Coherence

Does it feel authentically RDR 2? Does it engage meaningfully with the game's themes?

Mechanical Plausibility

Could this mission actually work within the game's existing systems?

Narrative Craft

Is the storytelling compelling? Are the characters interesting? Does the mission have an emotional or thematic payoff?

Presentation Quality

Are your visual and audio materials purposeful and well-integrated — not merely decorative?

Deliverable

Present Your Pitch to the Class

Present your mission design using PowerPoint, Google Slides, or any presentation software you prefer. Your presentation should run approximately 5 minutes and walk the class through each element of your mission as if you were pitching it to a development team.

Practical note: You will present from the classroom PC, so plan ahead. Save your file to Box, email it to yourself, or upload it to Google Drive. If your presentation includes audio or video, test it in advance — embedded media can behave differently across machines. A backup PDF export is always a smart precaution.
