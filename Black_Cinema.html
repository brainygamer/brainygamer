The Long Story of Black Cinema:
Seven Films, Eight Decades

Here's the thing about African American cinema: it didn't start as an art movement or a cultural statement. It started as self-defense.

From almost the moment movies existed, Hollywood was using the screen to tell lies about Black people: lazy stereotypes, minstrel caricatures, and worst of all, Birth of a Nation in 1915, a film that literally helped revive the Ku Klux Klan by presenting Black men as animals and white supremacists as heroes. Black filmmakers looked at what the screen was doing to their communities and said: we have to take this back. Everything that follows flows from that original act of resistance.

The Blood of Jesus (1941) — Spencer Williams

The moment This is the era of "race films," movies made by Black filmmakers, for Black audiences, shown in segregated theaters that Hollywood didn't even bother to serve. The country is still living under Jim Crow. World War II is starting. Black Americans are being asked to fight for a democracy that doesn't include them.

Why it matters Spencer Williams was a one-man film industry. He wrote, directed, and starred in this movie on almost no money, shooting in Texas with an all-Black cast and crew. The film is a folk religious allegory: a woman is accidentally shot, her soul wanders between heaven and hell, and the whole thing plays out with the imagery, music, and spiritual feeling of the Black church. It's raw and strange and completely alive. Hollywood wasn't making anything like it, because Hollywood wasn't thinking about Black spiritual life at all.

The take-away Race films like this one weren't consolation prizes. They were proof of concept: Black artists could control their own images, tell their own stories, and find their own audiences. Spencer Williams was doing in 1941 what independent filmmakers are still fighting to do today. And the fact that you've probably never heard of him tells you something important about whose history gets remembered.

What happened between 1941 and 1972?

The race film era died out as television rose and as integration (complicated, incomplete, contested) changed what Black audiences expected from Hollywood. Studios started casting Black stars: Sidney Poitier became famous playing dignified, respectable characters designed to make white audiences comfortable. For a while, that felt like progress. Then the 1960s exploded. Martin Luther King was assassinated. Cities burned. Black Power replaced "we shall overcome" as the defining mood of young Black America. And a generation of young people looked at Sidney Poitier being dignified and polite on screen and said: that's not us. That's not our rage. That's not our streets.

Super Fly (1972) — Gordon Parks Jr.

The moment Welcome to Blaxploitation, one of the most complicated chapters in all of American film history. Hollywood studios, losing money and audiences to television, discovered that Black urban audiences would turn out in huge numbers for movies that reflected their world. The problem was that most of these studios were still run by white executives, and "reflecting the Black world" often meant glamorizing crime, sex, and violence in ways that horrified many Black community leaders. The NAACP formally protested Blaxploitation films.

Why it matters Super Fly sits right at the center of that debate and refuses to let you feel comfortable. The hero, Youngblood Priest, is a cocaine dealer trying to get out of the drug trade, and the film is honest that the system has made this his best available option. The real villains turn out to be corrupt white cops. The soundtrack, by Curtis Mayfield, is a masterpiece of soul music that comments on the story with more moral complexity than the images alone can carry. This is a film arguing, in coded and stylish language, that the whole system is rigged.

The take-away Blaxploitation gets criticized, fairly, for trafficking in stereotypes. But there's something real here too. These films put Black faces at the center of the screen as powerful, sexual, streetwise, and dangerous. For young Black audiences in 1972, seeing that was electric. The question of whether representation that comes with those costs is worth it is a debate still happening right now, every time a controversial Black film gets released.

What happened between 1972 and 1978?

Blaxploitation burned bright and burned out fast. By the mid-70s, the studios had moved on. The brief window closed. And a young filmmaker named Charles Burnett decided that what Black cinema needed wasn't glamour or box office. It needed to look honestly at ordinary Black life and not flinch.

Killer of Sheep (1978) — Charles Burnett

The moment Burnett made this film as his graduate thesis at UCLA, where a group of young Black filmmakers, later called the L.A. Rebellion, were studying world cinema and asking what it could teach them about telling Black American stories without Hollywood's distortions. They were inspired by Italian neorealism, by African cinema, by the idea that you could make a film about poor people with no money, nonprofessional actors, and real locations, and end up with something truer than anything a studio could manufacture.

Why it matters Killer of Sheep follows Stan, a slaughterhouse worker in Watts, through a series of quiet, disconnected moments in his life. Nothing much "happens" in the plot sense. Stan is exhausted. His marriage is strained. His neighborhood is worn down. Children play in rubble. And the whole film has a beauty and a sadness that is almost unbearable, because Burnett is saying: look at these people. Really look at them. This is what it costs to survive. It was shot for $10,000. It wasn't commercially released for almost thirty years because of music licensing issues. Critics now regularly list it among the greatest American films ever made.

The take-away Killer of Sheep didn't get seen when it was made. That's part of the story of Black cinema: brilliant work gets made against the odds, and then doesn't get distributed, doesn't get marketed, disappears. The film industry has gatekeepers at every stage, and those gatekeepers have historically not been Black. Charles Burnett made a masterpiece in his twenties and then spent decades being one of the most important filmmakers most people had never heard of.

What happened between 1978 and 1989?

The 1980s arrived with Ronald Reagan and a cultural mood that wanted to look away from poverty, race, and inequality. MTV launched. Blockbusters became the dominant Hollywood form. And in Black communities, crack cocaine hit like a bomb, devastating neighborhoods, exploding incarceration rates, tearing families apart. Hip-hop was being born in the rubble, turning that pain into art. Spike Lee was watching all of it.

Do the Right Thing (1989) — Spike Lee

The moment It's a sweltering summer day in Bedford-Stuyvesant, Brooklyn. The film was made the year after a young Black man named Yusef Hawkins was murdered in a white Brooklyn neighborhood just for being there. Racial tension in New York was at a breaking point. Spike Lee, already known for She's Gotta Have It, made a film about exactly that breaking point, and it made Hollywood extremely nervous.

Why it matters Do the Right Thing doesn't let you pick a side and feel good about it. It's funny, warm, full of incredible characters, and then it builds to a confrontation between the neighborhood and a white-owned pizzeria that ends in a death and a riot. Mookie, the delivery guy played by Lee himself, throws the trash can through the window that starts the riot. Was that the right thing to do? The film won't tell you. Lee ends by quoting both Martin Luther King and Malcolm X, setting them against each other. He's asking you to sit with the contradiction.

The take-away Universal Pictures was genuinely afraid this film would cause riots in theaters. That fear tells you more about Hollywood than it does about the film. Do the Right Thing was made by a Black filmmaker who had finally broken through to major studio resources, and he used those resources to make something that challenged, rather than comforted, white audiences. The film was not nominated for Best Picture at the Academy Awards. Driving Miss Daisy won instead. Hollywood honoring a comforting film about race over a challenging one is still talked about as one of the great Oscar scandals.

What happened between 1989 and 1991?

Almost nothing, time-wise. Everything, culturally. The L.A. Rebellion moment had passed. Spike Lee had proved that a Black director could be a mainstream event. And a generation of young filmmakers, many of them from the communities they were depicting, were getting access to cameras and finding distributors willing to take a chance. John Singleton was twenty-three years old when he made his first film.

Boyz n the Hood (1991) — John Singleton

The moment South Central Los Angeles. The crack epidemic. Gang violence. Over-policing. The film came out a year before the L.A. Riots, and the city was already a powder keg. Singleton grew up in this neighborhood. He went to USC film school and came back to make a movie about what he'd seen.

Why it matters Boyz n the Hood defined a genre, the hood film, and it did so with real love for its characters and real anger at the systems that crush them. The film's moral center is Furious Styles, played by Laurence Fishburne, a father who has moved his son Tre to the neighborhood specifically because he believes Black fathers have to be present in their sons' lives. The film is, underneath the violence, about fatherhood, responsibility, and survival as forms of political resistance.

The take-away John Singleton became the first Black filmmaker and the youngest person ever nominated for the Academy Award for Best Director for this film. He was twenty-three. The movie was made for $6 million and earned $57 million. It proved, again, that Black stories could find massive audiences, not just Black audiences, but universal ones. Hollywood always seems surprised by this, and always has been.

What happened between 1991 and 2016?

Twenty-five years. The 1990s gave us a wave of hood films, then a wave of Black romantic comedies, then a wave of prestige biopics. Denzel Washington and Will Smith became global superstars. The conversation about representation got louder and more complicated: more Black faces on screen, but how many Black directors? How many Black stories that weren't about pain, crime, or sports? Then came the 2010s, Obama, 12 Years a Slave, the emergence of #OscarsSoWhite. And a quiet filmmaker named Barry Jenkins, working from a tiny play by Tarell Alvin McCraney, made something nobody had ever quite seen before.

Moonlight (2016) — Barry Jenkins

The moment The #OscarsSoWhite movement had just called out the Academy for two consecutive years of all-white acting nominees. There was real pressure on Hollywood to reckon with who was getting recognized, who was getting funded, whose stories were being told. And then Moonlight arrived: a film about a gay Black man growing up poor in Miami, told in three chapters of his life.

Why it matters Moonlight is doing something that almost no previous film in this history managed to do: centering Black interiority. Not action, not spectacle, not the clash of social forces, but the inner life of a person who has been told by almost everyone around him that he shouldn't exist as he is. The film is tender and almost unbearably beautiful, shot in blues and golds that feel like memory. And it's asking the audience to sit inside the emotional experience of someone very specific (poor, Black, gay, quiet) and recognize themselves.

The take-away Moonlight won Best Picture at the Academy Awards in the most dramatic moment in Oscar history, when La La Land was mistakenly announced as the winner and had to hand the award over. Beyond the drama, the win felt significant because of what the film is: not a big historical epic, not a civil rights story, not an action movie, just an intimate personal portrait of a Black life that had never been seen on screen before. Barry Jenkins proved you could make that film and win the highest award in American cinema.

What happened between 2016 and 2025?

A lot. Get Out had Jordan Peele using horror to talk about race in a way that reached 30 million people. Black Panther was a billion-dollar superhero film with an almost entirely Black cast that made kids cry in theaters because they'd never seen themselves like that. Judas and the Black Messiah, One Night in Miami, The Woman King. And then the streaming revolution, which opened new doors and closed others. Ryan Coogler, who made Fruitvale Station at twenty-six, then Creed, then Black Panther, kept growing. And then he made Sinners.

Sinners (2025) — Ryan Coogler

The moment We're living in a cultural moment when questions about Black history, memory, and erasure feel urgent and contested. Coogler made this film as a deeply personal project, set in 1932 Mississippi. It's a genre film (horror, blues, mythology) that's also about the origins of Black American music and the forces that have always tried to own, contain, or destroy Black creativity.

Why it matters What's remarkable about Sinners is that it uses genre (juke joint, supernatural horror, period atmosphere) to carry a serious argument about where Black American art comes from and what it costs. It connects the music of the Mississippi Delta to something ancient and spiritual, the way Spencer Williams did in The Blood of Jesus eighty-four years earlier. And it's made by a filmmaker with the resources of a major studio and the creative control of an independent, because Coogler negotiated to own the film himself.

The take-away Ryan Coogler owns Sinners. He retained the rights. That's not a small thing. It's the culmination of eighty years of Black filmmakers fighting for control of their own images and stories. Spencer Williams making race films in Texas couldn't have imagined it. But there's a straight line from his self-sufficiency to Coogler's. The fight has always been about who gets to tell the story and who profits from it.

The Whole Arc, In a Few Sentences

Black cinema begins with self-defense, taking back the image that Hollywood was weaponizing. It moves through periods of outsider independence (race films, the L.A. Rebellion), through complicated compromises with the mainstream (Blaxploitation), through explosive breakthroughs (Spike Lee, John Singleton), toward something that looks, slowly and incompletely, like creative ownership. The films on your list span horror and comedy, gritty realism and mythic beauty. But they share something: they were all made by people who had to fight harder than their white counterparts to get the camera in their hands, to get the film in front of an audience, to get recognized when they succeeded.

The story isn't over. It's not a triumphant march toward progress. It's a cycle of breakthroughs followed by new obstacles, of doors opened and then narrowed again. What these seven films give you is a way to see that cycle clearly, from the inside, through the eyes of artists who refused to disappear.
