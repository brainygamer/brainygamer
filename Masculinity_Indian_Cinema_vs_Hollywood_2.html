Comparative Framework

The Hollywood Model
What a man can do alone

I

The Stoic Loner

The Western gunslinger, the action hero. Power through isolation and physical competence. Identity forged in separation from society.

II

The Self-Made Man

The entrepreneur narrative. Aspirational self-sufficiency as the engine of masculine identity. Success measured by individual agency.

III

The Romantic Lead

Emotional arcs subordinated to conquest structures. Vulnerability as a recent complication, not a default mode.

The underlying grammar privileges individual agency, physical competence, and aspirational self-sufficiency.

Comparative Framework

The Bollywood Model
Masculinity through belonging

Networks of Obligation

Masculinity embedded within duty to family, community, and romantic ideals coded as devotional rather than acquisitive.

Emotional Openness

The hero weeps openly, dances unselfconsciously. Power derives not from isolation but from depth of feeling and belonging.

Collective Purpose

The masala hero fights for a village, a family, a nation — asserting relational identity rather than rugged individualism.

Performative Display

Masculinity is theatricalized and communally validated in ways Hollywood often codes as excessive or camp.

A Specific Case

Superboys
of Malegaon

Directed by Reema Kagti · 2012

Nasir Shaikh and his collaborators remake Hollywood and Bollywood blockbusters with zero-budget ingenuity — redefining heroism away from both the lone wolf and the family patriarch.

Superboys of Malegaon

Redefining the Hero

01

Aspiration Through Imitation

Remaking Superman, Sholay, Wanted with cardboard and ingenuity. Masculinity defined not by physical dominance but by the creative audacity of the attempt itself.

They're not strong or wealthy or conventionally powerful — they're resourceful, collaborative, and driven by imagination.

Heroism = creative audacity, not physical power
02

Community as Identity

Unlike Hollywood's hero who separates from society, the Malegaon filmmakers derive identity entirely from their town. Their films are for Malegaon, screened in Malegaon, celebrated in Malegaon.

Masculinity here is inseparable from civic participation and communal joy.

Closer to the Indian model, but inflected with working-class specificity
Superboys of Malegaon

Subverting the Spectacle

03

Vulnerability as Strength

The documentary doesn't hide the poverty, the limited resources, the occasional ridiculousness of the enterprise. The men are openly emotional about their work and their town.

This is neither Hollywood's controlled vulnerability nor Bollywood's melodramatic intensity — it's something more quotidian and arguably more honest.

Authenticity beyond either mainstream tradition
04

Parody as Masculine Agency

By remaking blockbusters with cardboard sets and visible wires, the filmmakers implicitly critique the hypermasculine spectacles of both industries.

The "superboy" isn't the man with superpowers — it's the man with a camera and a story to tell. A democratization of masculine heroism.

The hero is behind the camera, not in front of it
The Broader Takeaway

A third space where masculinity is defined by creative labor, community embeddedness, and the willingness to look foolish in pursuit of something meaningful.
