A Modest Proposal for Theater (heh)

April 2026

Producing musical theater in a small, all-male department is ridiculously hard, and the standard playbook wasn't designed with us in mind. Rather than continuing to improvise solutions show by show, this proposal suggests we step back and design a model that actually fits our situation.

  1. Choose the Right Shows
  2. Build a Real Partnership with Another School
  3. Formalize the Music Department Relationship
  4. Right-Size the Production Model
I

Choose the Right Shows

The single highest-leverage decision we make is repertoire. Many of our recurring difficulties — finding enough actors, managing loads, crew and shop strain — are significantly affected by what show we select. If we're going to do a musical on a regular cycle, the choice of material has to be deliberate, not aspirational.

Three viable approaches, which can be mixed depending on the moment:

Male-Weighted Repertoire

There is a richer canon of male-skewing and all-male musicals than is commonly assumed. Chamber musicals and two-handers open up further options.

Working · Sweeney Todd · The Full Monty · Tick Tick… Boom! · Floyd Collins · Spring Awakening

Gender-Flexible Casting as Artistic Policy

Instead of treating cross-gender or non-traditional casting as a workaround, we could own it as a deliberate artistic choice — putting us in the same conversation as some of the most exciting theater happening right now. Framing this as our approach rather than a compromise gives our program a distinct identity that could even help with recruiting.

Company · Cabaret · Into the Woods

Original or Devised Work

Longer-term, the most sustainable musical theater program might be one that creates its own work: small-scale, original, built around our ensemble. This eliminates licensing costs and is native to our resources. It also represents a genuine collaborative opportunity with Music.

II

Build a Real Partnership with Another School

Every production, we find ourselves renegotiating informally, hoping the right people are still available and willing. A formal Memorandum of Understanding (MOU) with a program like DePauw's Institute of Music would give us something to actually rely on.

An MOU doesn't need to be elaborate — two pages, signed by both department chairs, covering:

A

Audition access: their students may audition for our productions, with appropriate billing and recognition.

B

Academic credit: how their students receive recognition at their institution (independent study, practicum hours, etc.).

C

Pit orchestra: whether their Music program can reliably supply players, and under what terms.

The goal is to move from "we know some people over there" to "we have a production partner." That shift requires a document and two signatures.

III

Formalize the Music Department Relationship

The most important question this proposal raises is also the trickiest: what are we actually asking our colleague in Music to do, and what does Wabash owe them for doing it?

The current setup — where musical direction runs on goodwill and personal generosity — isn't sustainable, and it isn't fair. It puts the whole weight of that relationship on individuals rather than on any real interdepartmental structure, and it means the musical's future depends on whoever happens to be available and willing in a given year.

Consider one of the following remedies:

Stipend A formal stipend for musical direction, comparable to what we would pay an outside contractor.
Course Release A workload adjustment that recognizes the production as part of their formally assigned duties.
Joint Agreement A departmental agreement designating the musical as a shared production, with both Theater and Music formally invested in its success.

The third option is worth pursuing seriously. If our Music colleague is a co-owner of the production rather than a service provider to Theater, their chair has institutional reason to protect that time. The musical becomes something Music does, not something Music helps Theater with.

A related possibility: could the musical be timed to a course? A musical theater practicum cross-listed between Theater and Music, generating enrollment credit for both departments, would provide the institutional justification for faculty time on both sides.

IV

Right-Size the Production Model

A full proscenium musical with elaborate scenery, full orchestra, and weeks of rehearsal is expensive and exhausting because that model was designed for theater companies with more resources than we have. A tiered production model might serve us better.

Alternating Years

The Chamber Production

Minimal set · Piano + 2–3 players · Near-memorized

A concert staging costs a fraction of a full production and can be artistically equal to it if the material is right. This is not a lesser version of the musical; it is a different and entirely legitimate form. It also serves as a low-stakes way to build our partnership with the coed university before committing to a full production together.

Every Two Years (or so)

The Full Production

Proper design · Fuller orchestra · Complete technical investment

The full production remains the centerpiece. But it lands better, and is produced more sustainably, when it alternates with a lighter-touch year that keeps the work alive without depleting us. This rhythm also gives students more consistent exposure to musical theater across their four years.
